Statistics

CBA Investigation Practice
Ordinary 6 questions · 28 marks total
📊
📘 What is this topic?

CBA 2 at The 3 Amigos Tuition Centre: POSE question→COLLECT data→REPRESENT→ANALYSE→COMMUNICATE. Good question: specific, measurable, has a COMPARISON.

💡 Analogy

A statistical investigation is science with numbers instead of test tubes. Start with a HYPOTHESIS. Collect data. Analyse. Conclude: does data SUPPORT or REFUTE your hypothesis? Let the DATA speak!

🔍 Worked Example
Well-posed vs poorly-posed CBA question
  1. POOR: 'Do students like sport?' (yes/no, not measurable)
  2. BETTER: 'Do 1st year students at The 3 Amigos Tuition Centre spend >2h/week on sport compared to music?'
  3. Why better: specific GROUP, specific VARIABLES, COMPARISON
  4. Analysis: compare mean, median and IQR for both variables
  5. Always state LIMITATIONS: sample size, response bias
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Never draw conclusion WITHOUT citing your data values
  • Small samples (n<30) limit generalisation — mention this!
  • Response bias: students may answer based on what they THINK they should say
📌 Key Formulae
CBA cycle: Pose→Collect→Represent→Analyse→CommunicateLimitations: sample size, response bias, self-report accuracy
🎯 SEC Exam Insight: CBA 2 at The 3 Amigos Tuition Centre is your statistical investigation. The most common weakness: vague conclusions. Always write: 'My data shows X because mean hours sport (Y) > mean hours music (Z).'

Q1 Pose a suitable statistical investigation question for a first-year student. Identify data type and whether to use primary or secondary data. 4 marks
Your Answer
💡 Make sure the question is specific, measurable, and has a comparison.
✅ Model Answer: E.g. 'Do 1st year students spend more time on social media on weekdays vs weekends?' Primary data: survey. Continuous numerical data.
Q2 Describe three different sampling methods. Which would you recommend for fairness? 4 marks
Your Answer
💡 Think about how to ensure every group in the year is fairly represented.
✅ Model Answer: Random (each person equally likely), Systematic (every kth person), Stratified (proportional from each class). Recommend: stratified — fairest representation.
Q3 Music hours/day: 1,2,3,3,4,4,5,3,4,2. Sport hours: 1,2,2,2,3,3,2,1,3,2. Calculate mean and median for each and compare. 5 marks
Your Answer
💡 Calculate mean=sum÷count and median=middle value (after ordering) for each.
✅ Model Answer: Music: mean=3.1, median=3. Sport: mean=2.1, median=2. Students spend more time on music (higher mean and median).
Q4 Using Q3 data, describe how to display the two distributions for comparison. 5 marks
Your Answer
💡 Choose a display that lets you compare both datasets simultaneously.
✅ Model Answer: Back-to-back stem-and-leaf or side-by-side bar chart. Music values cluster higher (3−4h); Sport lower (2h). Music shows more spread.
Q5 From Q3, P(>3hrs music) and P(>2hrs sport). Assuming independence, find P(both). 5 marks
Your Answer
💡 Count values >3 in the music data and >2 in the sport data.
✅ Model Answer: P(music>3)=4/10=0.4. P(sport>2)=3/10=0.3. P(both)=0.12
Q6 List THREE limitations of the Q3 investigation and suggest improvements. 5 marks
Your Answer
💡 Think about sample size, data accuracy, and time period.
✅ Model Answer: 1. Small sample (n=10) → increase to 60+. 2. Self-reported data may be inaccurate → use time-tracking app. 3. One-day snapshot → sample over multiple weeks.
